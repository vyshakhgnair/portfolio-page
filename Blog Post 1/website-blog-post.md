# From a CA's Son to AI/ML Engineer: My Journey Into the Future I Chose Before I Understood It

**By Vyshakh** | *April 2026* | *Data Science · AI/ML · Career*

---

> *"I didn't fully understand the future when I chose it — but I believed in it, worked for it, and grew into it."*

---

## The Question That Started Everything

Right after my 10th grade, my father — a Chartered Accountant — sat me down and asked a simple question:

**"Do you want to follow my path?"**

I said no.

Not out of rebellion. Not because it wasn't a great career. But because something deeper was pulling me — toward **Computer Science, Mathematics, and Physics**. I couldn't explain it then. I just knew.

My father, to his credit, didn't push back. Instead, he gave me the most important advice I've ever received:

> "If you're choosing Computer Science, find a field that has a future."

That single sentence sent me down a rabbit hole that changed everything.

---

## Discovering AI Before I Could Even Spell "Transformer"

It was around **2017** when I first came across Artificial Intelligence. The paper *"Attention Is All You Need"* was quietly reshaping the world of deep learning. I stumbled upon it and tried to read it.

Did I understand it? **Not at all.**

But something clicked — a gut feeling stronger than logic:

> *"This is going to be the future."*

I was 16. I didn't know what a neural network was. I couldn't tell you the difference between supervised and unsupervised learning. But I made a decision that day that shaped the next seven years of my life.

I decided to follow Artificial Intelligence — even before I understood it.

---

## The Academic Phase: Building the Foundation

I chose Computer Science in +1 and +2, taught myself Python, and started building the fundamentals. I even discovered *Machine Learning* by Andrew Ng during my 12th — though I'll be honest, I only got partway through it at the time.

Then came the entrance exams.

- ❌ JEE didn't work out the way I hoped.
- ✅ I got a strong rank in the **Kerala Engineering Entrance Exam** and secured my BTech seat.

Around the same time, something exciting happened: **IIT Madras launched its BSc in Data Science** program — an online, flexible degree anyone could enroll in alongside their primary studies. I looked at it and thought:

*"This should be manageable."*

I was completely wrong about that.

---

## Managing Two Worlds Simultaneously

What followed was the most demanding phase of my academic life.

I was simultaneously:
- Completing my **BTech** degree
- Pursuing a **BSc in Data Science from IIT Madras**
- Self-studying AI/ML concepts on the side

The balancing act was relentless. I had to strategize every semester — how many IIT courses to take based on my BTech load. There were weeks where sleep was a luxury. College events? Often missed. Time with friends? Carefully rationed.

But I made one promise to myself:

> "I will not quit in the middle."

I didn't.

In 2025, I **graduated from IIT Madras** with my BSc in Data Science — completed entirely in parallel with my BTech. It is one of the things I am most proud of, not because of the degree itself, but because of what it taught me about discipline, sacrifice, and the compound effect of showing up every single day.

---

## Falling in Love With Machine Learning — For Real This Time

During college, I went back to Andrew Ng's *Machine Learning* course — and this time, I went deep.

I wasn't just running code. I was understanding **why** it worked. The math behind gradient descent. The intuition behind regularization. The architecture decisions in neural networks. I built things, broke them, rebuilt them.

Then I moved into **Deep Learning** — CNNs, RNNs, transformers. Then into **LLMs and Generative AI** — RAG pipelines, fine-tuning, prompt engineering.

By this point, AI wasn't just a career choice. It was something I genuinely loved.

---

## Building, Failing, Growing: The Real Education

The next phase was where theory met reality — and the gap between them was humbling.

Here's what that phase looked like:

- 🏆 **Won hackathons** — learned to build fast, iterate faster
- 🚀 **Co-founded a startup** with a team that scaled to a **₹5 crore valuation** — learned what it means to build something from nothing
- 🤖 **Became Founding Engineer at Corr Robotics** — shipped real AI systems in production
- 📄 **Published research** — IEEE and journal contributions
- 🎓 **Built AI systems for students and learning platforms** — saw firsthand how AI can change education

Each of these experiences taught me something no course could: **how to build, own, and defend decisions under pressure.**

Later, due to personal circumstances, I made the difficult decision to step down from the startup. It was one of the hardest calls I've made. But sometimes, knowing when to step back is as important as knowing when to push forward.

---

## The Lowest Phase: Placements

Then came placements — and it humbled me in ways I didn't expect.

On paper, I had:
- Dual degrees
- Real startup experience at scale
- Published research
- Production-level AI systems

And yet — rejection after rejection.

I felt lost. I questioned everything. Was my work not good enough? Was I presenting myself wrong? Was the market just unfair?

Probably a mix of all three.

But the answer wasn't to stop.

---

## The Turning Point: Analyze, Improve, Repeat

Instead of spiraling, I did something simple — and it made all the difference:

1. **I analyzed every rejection** — What went wrong? Where did I lose them?
2. **I improved my approach** — Communication, positioning, how I talked about my work
3. **I kept applying** — Again. And again. And again.

This phase tested something more important than technical skill: **resilience**. The ability to face repeated failure and still show up the next day with the same energy.

---

## The Breakthrough

Towards the end of placement season, something shifted.

**Multiple offers came in.**

After months of silence and rejection, the doors opened — not just one, but several. I chose to join **Rappit as an AI/ML Engineer**, because it was exactly the domain I had believed in since 2017.

Working on the technology I chose as a teenager, before I even understood it.

That felt like full circle.

---

## What I Want You to Take From This

If you're a student, an early-career professional, or someone standing at a crossroads — here's what this journey taught me:

### 1. You don't need to understand everything to start
I followed AI before I understood transformers, neural networks, or even basic calculus applications. The commitment came first. The understanding followed.

### 2. Failure early is not failure permanently
JEE didn't work out. Placements were brutal. Neither defined the outcome. Every setback was data — I just had to learn to read it.

### 3. Real growth happens in the difficult phases
The dual-degree grind, the startup pressure, the placement rejections — these weren't obstacles to my growth. They *were* the growth.

### 4. Opportunities come to those who persist
If I had stopped applying after the 10th, 20th, or 30th rejection, I wouldn't be where I am. Persistence is the most underrated skill in any career.

### 5. Start before you feel ready
I started a startup before I felt ready. I became a Founding Engineer before I felt qualified. I published research before I felt like a "researcher."
You become ready by doing — not by waiting.

---

## One Last Thing

I want to acknowledge the people without whom none of this would have been possible:

- My **teachers at both institutions** — who supported me through clashing deadlines and never let me fall through the cracks
- My **friends** — who caught me up when I was buried in work and kept me sane through the chaos
- And most importantly, my **parents** — who believed in me at every step, especially when I didn't believe in myself 🙏

---

The road was long. The nights were late. The doubt was real.

But so was the dream.

And the dream won.
